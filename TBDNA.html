TB DNA Extraction Electrode

I spent my second quarter of senior design in South Africa from January to March of 2020. There, I spent time learning about the culture, history, and healthcare system of the country as well as completeing a design project in the Biomedical Engineering field. One partner and I were tasked with improving the method used by the Tygerberg Genomics Research Group for for separating DNA and RNA from Tuberculosis cells. The central goal was to make the process faster while maintaining a similarly high yeild of DNA from cells because the lab needed to complete this process everyday and improved effiecny would allow them to complete more research and help more patients.

The Tygerberg Genomics Research Group is a laboratory group at Stellenbosch University's Tygerberg medical campus in Cape Town, South Africa. Along with separating DNA, this group takes sputum samples from patients with Tuberculosis, cultures it to grow the bacteria cells, separates the DNA, and sends it to a lab in the US thst performs Whole Genome Sequencing (WGS). WGS can help researches better understand the disease and develop drugs to treat it, a very important measure for a country with such wide spread TB.

We set about learning the current method of DNA extraction by performing one in their lab space. It took a little less than two and a half days to complete the extraction, with plenty of waiting time ni between. We were about to target the part of the process after the cells have been lysed (broken open) when the DNA is actually being removed from the rest of the debris as a key time to increase efficiency. Through research, we realized that the negatively charged phosphate backbone of a DNA or RNA strand is one of the few negatively charged parts of a lysed cell. By using a positively charged electrode, we could extract only the DNA from the cell debris and then reverse the polarity to expell it into a separate fluid. This would reduce the number necessary added chemicals by half making it more affordable and more simple and make the process 10% faster.

In terms of design, the quarter only afforded us the time to identify a way to increase efficiency and do some design work before we passed the project on to a local Master's student. After some further user observation we determined that desiging electrode in the shape of micro pipette, something the lab technicians were very familiar with using. Furthermore, we worked with a robptics research group to design the electrode to have a gold body with a nafion coating to ensure it is both highly conductive and inert so as to keep the DNA and highly unstable RNA intact. The two buttons shown on the design are intended to be for both DNA collection and release respectively. They are located in the same place are the collection button on a micro pipette. We hope our deisgn with be built, tested, and implemented as soon as possible.
